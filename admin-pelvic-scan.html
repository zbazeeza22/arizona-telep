Young-Burgess Classification

Clinical Reference

Select an injury mechanism to view subtypes, stability, and clinical details.

Key CT Findings

Transverse sacral fracture (anterior cortex only). Ipsilateral pubic rami fractures (superior and/or inferior). No posterior ligament disruption. Minimal or no displacement.

Mechanism

Lateral force causing internal rotation of hemipelvis. Common in side-impact MVCs and falls onto the side.

Clinical Significance

Most common pelvic ring injury (~60%). Posterior ring intact. Generally nonoperative, weight-bearing as tolerated. Low hemorrhage risk. Monitor for associated urogenital injuries.

Key CT Findings

Crescent (iliac wing) fracture with posterior iliac disruption. Ipsilateral pubic rami fractures. SI joint partially disrupted anteriorly. Internal rotation deformity of hemipelvis.

Mechanism

Higher-energy lateral compression. Force drives iliac wing medially, causing posterior iliac crescent fracture with partially intact posterior ligaments.

Clinical Significance

Rotationally unstable, vertically stable. Often requires operative fixation. Evaluate for L5 nerve root injury. Higher transfusion requirements than LC-I. Crescent fragment size determines surgical approach.

Key CT Findings

Ipsilateral LC-I or LC-II pattern PLUS contralateral open-book injury. Widened contralateral SI joint or sacral fracture. Contralateral symphysis diastasis or rami fractures. "Windswept pelvis" appearance.

Mechanism

Lateral compression on one side with rollover or contralateral external rotation. High-energy (rollover MVC, crush). Contralateral hemipelvis externally rotates as ipsilateral side compresses.

Clinical Significance

Completely unstable: rotationally and vertically. High mortality. Significant hemorrhage risk -- apply pelvic binder, consider angioembolization. Requires anterior and posterior ring fixation. High association with visceral and vascular injuries. Evaluate for Morel-Lavallee lesion.

Key CT Findings

Pubic symphysis diastasis <2.5 cm. No posterior ring disruption. SI joints normal. Anterior SI ligaments intact or minimally stretched.

Mechanism

Low-energy AP force causing external rotation. Motorcycle straddling injuries, direct anterior blow.

Clinical Significance

Mechanically stable. Symphyseal ligaments partially torn, but sacrospinous, sacrotuberous, and posterior SI ligaments intact. Typically nonoperative. Low hemorrhage risk.

Key CT Findings

Pubic symphysis diastasis >2.5 cm ("open book"). Widened anterior SI joint. Disrupted sacrospinous and sacrotuberous ligaments. Posterior SI ligaments intact. Look for avulsion fragments at sacrospinous insertion.

Mechanism

Moderate-energy AP compression. Classic "open book" mechanism. Motorcycle collisions, head-on MVCs, direct anterior crush.

Clinical Significance

Rotationally unstable, vertically stable. "Open book" increases pelvic volume, causing significant retroperitoneal hemorrhage. Requires emergent pelvic binder. Operative anterior plating typically required. Average 4-6 units pRBC. Evaluate for bladder and urethral injury.

Key CT Findings

Complete symphyseal disruption. Complete SI joint disruption (anterior and posterior ligaments). Lateral sacral fracture may replace SI dislocation. Hemipelvis displaced laterally and/or posteriorly. Evaluate for vascular contrast extravasation on CTA.

Mechanism

High-energy AP compression. Complete disruption of all pelvic ligaments. Severe MVCs, pedestrian struck, crush injuries.

Clinical Significance

Completely unstable: rotationally and vertically. Mortality up to 20%. Massive hemorrhage from posterior venous plexus and internal iliac branches. Emergent binder, resuscitation, consider preperitoneal packing or angioembolization. Requires anterior and posterior fixation. Highest association with concomitant abdominal and vascular injuries.

Key CT Findings

Vertical displacement of hemipelvis (cranial or caudal shift). Symphysis disruption or pubic rami fractures anteriorly. Complete posterior disruption: SI dislocation, sacral fracture, or iliac wing fracture. Vertical offset >1 cm on coronal reformats. Associated L5 transverse process fractures.

Mechanism

Axial loading through one hemipelvis. Falls from height landing on one extremity. High-speed motorcycle collisions. Force drives hemipelvis superiorly relative to sacrum.

Clinical Significance

Completely unstable in all planes. Mortality ~25%, massive transfusion requirements. Highest rate of lumbosacral plexus injury (up to 50%). Requires skeletal traction and definitive anterior + posterior fixation. Evaluate for open fracture (perineal laceration = Faringer Zone III). Associated with high ISS and multisystem trauma.

Key CT Findings

Features of multiple injury patterns (e.g., LC on one side with APC or VS contralaterally). Bilateral posterior ring disruption of differing types. Asymmetric displacement. Complex sacral fracture morphology that does not fit a single mechanism.

Mechanism

Multiple simultaneous or sequential force vectors. Highest-energy mechanisms: pedestrian struck, crush injuries, high-speed MVCs with rollover.

Clinical Significance

Completely unstable. Highest ISS and mortality among pelvic fractures. Each injury component must be addressed independently. Prioritize hemorrhage control (binder, angioembolization, preperitoneal packing). Individualized surgical planning required. Multidisciplinary trauma team essential. Highest rates of associated head, thoracic, and abdominal injuries.

AO/OTA Classification (61 - Pelvic Ring)

Clinical Reference

Click a type to expand subtypes. Color indicates stability: green = stable, yellow = partially unstable, red = unstable.

A1 -- Avulsion fracture Stable

ASIS, AIIS, or ischial tuberosity avulsion. Common in young athletes. Apophyseal avulsions in adolescents. Nonoperative unless displaced >2 cm.

A2 -- Iliac wing fracture (Duverney) Stable

Isolated iliac wing fracture not involving the pelvic ring. Direct blow mechanism. Posterior ring intact. Nonoperative with protected weight-bearing. Monitor for abdominal wall hernia through defect.

A3 -- Transverse sacral or coccyx fracture Stable

Isolated transverse sacral fracture below SI joints, or coccyx fracture. Fall onto buttocks. Does not disrupt pelvic ring. Nonoperative. Evaluate for cauda equina symptoms if displaced.

B1 -- Open book (external rotation) Rot. Unstable

Unilateral external rotation. Symphysis diastasis or anterior rami fracture with partial posterior disruption. Anterior SI ligaments torn, posterior intact. Corresponds to APC-II. Anterior fixation indicated if diastasis >2.5 cm.

B2 -- Lateral compression (internal rotation) Rot. Unstable

Unilateral internal rotation. Ipsilateral rami fractures with posterior compression (sacral buckle or crescent fracture). Posterior SI ligaments partially intact. Corresponds to LC-II. Evaluate posterior fixation based on displacement.

B3 -- Bilateral (bilateral B1 or B2) Rot. Unstable

Bilateral rotationally unstable injury. Bilateral open book, bilateral LC, or contralateral combination ("bucket handle"). Higher instability than unilateral. Often requires bilateral operative stabilization. Higher hemorrhage risk.

C1 -- Unilateral complete disruption Unstable

Unilateral complete posterior ring disruption (SI dislocation, sacral fracture, or iliac fracture) with vertical displacement. Anterior ring disrupted. Corresponds to APC-III or VS. Requires anterior and posterior fixation. High hemorrhage risk.

C2 -- Bilateral, one side complete Unstable

One side complete (C-type), contralateral partial (B-type). Asymmetric instability. Must address both sides surgically. Corresponds to LC-III pattern. Higher ISS than unilateral C injuries.

C3 -- Bilateral complete disruption Unstable

Bilateral complete posterior disruption. Maximum instability. Highest mortality, massive hemorrhage, polytrauma. Emergent damage control: binder, resuscitation, angioembolization or preperitoneal packing. Staged definitive fixation. Evaluate for associated acetabular and spinal injuries.

Stability Assessment

Decision Support

Question 1 of up to 4

Is the posterior pelvic ring intact?

Evaluate sacrum, SI joints, and posterior ilium on axial and coronal CT images.

Question 2 of up to 4

Is there anterior ring disruption only?

Check for pubic rami fractures or symphysis diastasis without posterior involvement.

Question 2 of up to 4

Is the posterior disruption complete or incomplete?

Complete: full sacral fracture, SI joint dislocation, or iliac wing fracture through posterior cortex. Incomplete: partial posterior ring involvement.

Question 3 of up to 4

Is there rotational instability?

Assess for internal/external rotation deformity of the hemipelvis on axial CT. Look for widening or compression at the SI joint and pubic symphysis.

STABLE (Type A) Tile A

Posterior ring intact with isolated anterior ring disruption. The pelvic ring retains structural stability.

Immediate Management

  • • Weight-bearing as tolerated
  • • Serial imaging at 2 and 6 weeks to confirm stability
  • • Analgesic management and early mobilization
  • • DVT prophylaxis per institutional protocol
STABLE No Tile classification

No pelvic ring injury identified. Posterior ring intact and no anterior ring disruption. Pelvis is stable.

Immediate Management

  • • Weight-bearing as tolerated
  • • Serial imaging if clinical concern persists
  • • Consider MRI if occult fracture suspected
ROTATIONALLY UNSTABLE (Type B) Tile B

Incomplete posterior disruption with rotational instability. The hemipelvis can rotate but vertical stability is maintained by intact posterior ligaments.

Immediate Management

  • • Apply pelvic binder to reduce rotational displacement
  • • Restricted weight-bearing — non-weight-bearing on affected side
  • • Consider external fixation for persistent instability
  • • Orthopedic/trauma surgery consultation
  • • Monitor hemodynamic status — transfuse as indicated
PARTIALLY STABLE (Type B1) Tile B

Incomplete posterior disruption without significant rotational instability. Posterior ligamentous structures are partially intact, providing some residual stability.

Immediate Management

  • • Apply pelvic binder as a precaution
  • • Restricted weight-bearing — toe-touch only on affected side
  • • Serial imaging at 1, 2, and 6 weeks to confirm stability is maintained
  • • Orthopedic consultation for operative vs. conservative management decision
COMPLETELY UNSTABLE (Type C) Tile C

Complete posterior disruption with both rotational and vertical instability. The hemipelvis is completely destabilized. High risk of life-threatening hemorrhage.

Urgent — Immediate Action Required

  • • Apply pelvic binder IMMEDIATELY at the level of the greater trochanters
  • • Prepare for emergent intervention — activate trauma surgery team
  • • Assess hemodynamic status — initiate massive transfusion protocol if indicated
  • • Consider angioembolization if arterial blush on contrast CT
  • • Consider preperitoneal packing if hemodynamically unstable and no angiography access
  • • Strict non-weight-bearing — flat bed rest

Tile Classification Reference: A = Stable (posterior ring intact)  |  B = Rotationally unstable, vertically stable  |  C = Rotationally + vertically unstable
