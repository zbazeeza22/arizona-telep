This means getting cited in AI Overviews is now the single highest-leverage SEO activity for Arizona Telepsychiatry Clinic.

**Target Page:** New blog article (article-telepsychiatry-vs-therapy.html)

**H2 Heading:**

```
Telepsychiatry vs Therapy: What Is the Difference?
```

**Direct Answer Block (56 words):**
> Telepsychiatry is online psychiatric care provided by a psychiatrist or psychiatric nurse practitioner who can diagnose conditions, prescribe medication, and manage treatment plans via video. Therapy (or teletherapy) is talk-based treatment provided by a psychologist, counselor, or social worker focused on behavioral strategies and coping skills. Many patients benefit from both simultaneously.

**Supporting Content -- Comparison Table (implement as HTML table):**

| Feature | Telepsychiatry | Therapy (Teletherapy) |
|---------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Provider** | Psychiatrist or PMHNP | Psychologist, LCSW, LPC |
| **Can Prescribe Medication** | Yes | No |
| **Focus** | Diagnosis, medication management, biological treatment | Talk therapy, behavioral strategies, coping skills |
| **Session Length** | 15-60 minutes | 45-60 minutes |
| **Common Conditions** | ADHD, anxiety, depression, insomnia, bipolar | Anxiety, depression, trauma, relationship issues |
| **Delivery** | Secure video call | Secure video call |
| **Best For** | Patients needing medication or psychiatric evaluation | Patients needing ongoing talk-based support |

**Supporting Paragraph 1:**
> Telepsychiatry and therapy address mental health from different angles. A telepsychiatrist evaluates the biological and neurological aspects of conditions like ADHD, anxiety, and depression, and can prescribe and adjust medications accordingly. A therapist focuses on thought patterns, emotional regulation, and behavioral change through evidence-based techniques like CBT, DBT, or EMDR.

**Supporting Paragraph 2:**
> Research consistently shows that telepsychiatry produces treatment outcomes equivalent to in-person psychiatric care. A 2023 systematic review in the Journal of Telemedicine and Telecare found no significant difference in diagnostic reliability, patient satisfaction, or symptom improvement between virtual and in-person psychiatry. The same holds true for teletherapy versus in-person therapy.

## 10. GOOGLE AI OVERVIEW OPTIMIZATION TACTICS

Based on 2026 research, these are the specific tactics to get cited in Google's AI Overviews.

### Tactic 1: Direct Answer Blocks (Highest Priority)

Every target page must contain a "direct answer block" -- a self-contained paragraph of 40-60 words that directly and completely answers the question without requiring the reader to parse surrounding context.

**Implementation:** Use a CSS class `.snippet-answer` on these paragraphs for easy identification and schema markup. Place them immediately after the H2 question heading with no introductory filler.

**What NOT to do:**
- "Great question! Let's explore..." (filler)
- "In this article, we will discuss..." (meta-reference)
- "Many people wonder about..." (indirect)

**What TO do:**
- Start with the answer: "To get tested for ADHD in Arizona, schedule..."
- Start with yes/no: "Yes, you can get ADHD medication through..."

### Tactic 2: Comprehensive Topic Coverage

AI Overviews favor sources that cover a topic thoroughly enough to serve as a primary reference. The more of the AI Overview's answer that can be sourced from your single page, the more likely that page is to be cited.

**Implementation:**
- Each condition page should be 1,500-2,500 words minimum
- Cover the topic from multiple angles (symptoms, treatment, cost, process, FAQ)
- Include related subtopics (e.g., an anxiety page should mention panic attacks, GAD, social anxiety)
- Answer People Also Ask questions within the same page

### Tactic 3: E-E-A-T Signals (Critical for Health Content)

Google's quality rater guidelines give special scrutiny to YMYL (Your Money, Your Life) health content. AI Overviews strongly prefer citing sources with clear author expertise.

**Implementation:**
- Add author bylines with credentials (e.g., "Reviewed by [Provider Name], MD, Board-Certified Psychiatrist")
- Include a visible author bio section with medical credentials
- Add `MedicalWebPage` schema type to health content pages
- Link to provider profiles and credentials
- Include "Medically reviewed" or "Clinically reviewed" dates
- Cite peer-reviewed sources (NIH, Mayo Clinic, CDC) within content

### Tactic 4: Multi-Format Content

AI Overviews pull from paragraphs, lists, AND tables. Pages that contain all three formats have a higher probability of being cited for different query types.

**Implementation for each condition page:**
1. Paragraph: Direct answer block (40-60 words)
2. Bullet list: Symptoms, treatment options, or process steps
3. Numbered list: "When to see a provider" or "steps to get started"
4. Table: Cost comparison, treatment comparison, or medication comparison
5. Definition: Clear definition of the condition in the opening

### Tactic 5: Freshness Signals

AI Overviews prefer recently updated content, particularly for queries with regulatory or policy components.

**Implementation:**
- Add visible "Last updated: [date]" on every page
- Update regulatory information (DEA rules, Arizona laws) whenever changes occur
- Re-publish or update key articles quarterly
- Use `dateModified` in schema markup

### Tactic 6: Source Citation Within Content

Pages that cite authoritative external sources (NIH, CDC, Mayo Clinic, peer-reviewed journals) are more likely to be treated as trustworthy by AI Overviews.

**Implementation:**
- Cite 2-3 authoritative sources per page
- Link to .gov and .edu sources for regulatory claims
- Reference specific studies with publication year
- Use a "Sources" or "References" section at the bottom of each page

### Tactic 7: Structured Data Beyond FAQPage

Implement multiple schema types to maximize AI understanding.

```json
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "MedicalWebPage",
  "name": "ADHD Evaluation in Arizona | Arizona Telepsychiatry Clinic",
  "description": "...",
  "medicalAudience": {
    "@type": "Patient"
  },
  "lastReviewed": "2026-04-01",
  "reviewedBy": {
    "@type": "Physician",
    "name": "[Provider Name]",
    "medicalSpecialty": "Psychiatry"
  },
  "mainEntity": {
    "@type": "MedicalCondition",
    "name": "Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder",
    "alternateName": "ADHD"
  }
```
